Research Interest

My research focuses on urban governance as shaped by multiple actors, including public authorities, for-profit firms, nonprofit organizations, and individuals. In particular, I examine patterns and mechanisms of collaboration among actors and their implications for governance processes and outcomes.

More recently, my research has focused on how choices made by actors at different levels are shaped by institutional, relational, and contextual conditions.

  • Masters Thesis. (2018). A Study on the Reconceptualization of Sharing and a Case Analysis of the Sharing Economy. (Written in Korean; Korean title: : )
    • This study examines collaboration among individuals in urban contexts, focusing on the concept of sharing. Since the 2010s, sharing has emerged as a prominent rhetorical device in marketing and public policy, often associated with notions of community and collective action. Seoul, the first city to officially declare itself a Sharing City, actively promoted policies and initiatives aimed at fostering a sharing culture. However, the concept of sharing remains analytically ambiguous and has generated considerable debate. To address this ambiguity, the study clarifies the concept of sharing by developing an ideal-typical framework and applying it to a case analysis of car-sharing in Seoul. Drawing on literature from anthropology, sociology, and business studies, the analysis distinguishes key dimensions of sharingparticularly patterns of behavior (such as allocation and commoning practices) and states of possession and ownershipand examines their implications for understanding collaboration among individuals in urban settings..
