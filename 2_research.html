Research

Research Interest

My research focuses on urban governance as it emerges from interactions among multiple actors—including public authorities, for-profit firms, nonprofit organizations, and individuals—under varying institutional and contextual conditions. I examine how patterns of collaboration among these actors shape governance processes and outcomes.

More specifically, my recent work investigates how actors’ choices at different levels are conditioned by institutional arrangements, interorganizational relationships, and broader contextual environments.

Urban Shrinkage as a Key Context

Urban shrinkage—marked by sustained population loss, economic decline, and physical deterioration—constitutes a governing context rather than a discrete policy problem.

Rather than focusing on shrinkage as a discrete policy problem, this perspective shifts attention from policy instruments to how governance arrangements, collaboration, and conflict evolve under sustained demographic and structural constraints. While public administration has long examined government responses to fiscal stress and economic downturns, comparatively less attention has been paid to how such long-term demographic and structural change shapes governance choices and interorganizational relationships.

The studies presented below examine how demographic shifts shape governance dynamics. I focus empirically on South Korea, an early and extreme case of urban shrinkage, which allows examination of these dynamics under long-term structural constraints that are increasingly observed in advanced economies:

Community-Level Collaboration Facilitated by Local Governments

  • Master’s Thesis (2018). A Study on the Reconceptualization of Sharing and a Case Analysis of the Sharing Economy (Written in Korean; Korean title: 공유의 재개념화와 공유경제 사례분석: 서울시 자동차 공유사례를 중심으로)
    • This study examines collaboration at the community level, focusing on the concept of “sharing.” Since the 2010s, “sharing” has emerged as a prominent rhetorical device in marketing and public policy, often associated with notions of community and collective action. Seoul, the first city to officially declare itself a “Sharing City” in 2012, actively promoted policies and initiatives aimed at fostering a sharing culture. However, the concept of sharing remains analytically ambiguous and contested. To address this ambiguity, the study develops an ideal-typical framework of sharing and applies it to a case analysis of car-sharing in Seoul. Drawing on literature from anthropology, sociology, and business studies, the analysis distinguishes key dimensions of sharing—particularly patterns of behavior (including allocation and commoning practices) and states of possession and ownership—and examines their implications for understanding collaboration in urban communities.
